Exploration: Identity, values and strengths

Thoughts on some of the motivations and values I'm bringing to this learning experience

28 February 2025

You can picture it, a crossroad you probably find yourself at a lot - one road leads down an easier path, but towards a person you don't really enjoy being, and a second path that's harder, but leads you to act as the kind of person you want to be. The ethical choice is effing hard! Imagine you're hiring some extra help at work for Christmas and your team mate has told his daughter to apply. You end up hiring her, instead of someone with more experience. This happened to me recently. I had decided to prioritize personal ties over merit. I was willing to put more work into training and be able to help a friend out.

---

For me, a high compliment is someone saying I remind them of a person I know or admire. It might be that I have generosity like my dad, funny like my friend or business savvy like my boss. Truth is, people rub off on us all the time and it's kinda nice to think about who has best shaped your identity and values (cue warm fuzzies). I learned the value of community early on with my family, growing up in a small town. I think the kitchen table is one of the most important pieces of furniture in a home. It's where I've experienced community most vividly and is the place many of my values were cemented as a kid and continue as an adult - the importance of laughter, caring for visitors, accepting a challenging viewpoint, being willing to stand up for your own, making a decent cup of tea...as I said, the IMPORTANT stuff! More recently, my values have been shaped working nearly ten years on cruise ships. Working alongside thirty-plus nationalities, surrounded by water, can be both challenging and rewarding. You learn a lot about people, everything from how their culture shapes their world view to how you both can curve your accents to understand one another. You learn the value of creating family when away from your own and most importantly, how to work hard, play hard!

---

If I'm honest, I love a good quote. I can't help it, I grew up when the wave of self-help books and spirituality was at its gnarliest. A couple come to mind that help my approach to learning and highlight some of my strengths:

"I am a human being, nothing human can be alien to me" - if you accept this, then you can never look at someone's rude behaviour and say 'oh I'd never do that...'. On the flip side, if someone does something great and seemingly impossible, you take comfort in the fact that you have everything in you, just as they do, to be able to achieve the same things. I try to keep a positive outlook like this which puts me in a helpful position to learn.

"Life is long, if you're lucky" - Essentially, don't sweat the small stuff. I keep a big picture view to get through the challenges of the day. Chances are you'll look back after a day, month, year and wonder what the fuss was all about. Be grateful for what you're problem-solving and learning today, tomorrow isn't a guarantee.

"You can have it all, you just can't have it all at once" - Have patience. Success requires all kinds of sacrifice with failure pretty much built into the process when you choose to dream big. I'm less shaken by making mistakes with this mindset and try my best to see success possible for anything you're willing to stick with.

---

Waxing poetic about myself is good and all but we all have our limits day-to-day. Sometimes you don't think you can achieve anything great. Sometimes you are sweating the small stuff. Sometimes you are impatient for success. There's outside influences that can hinder progress - a massive day at work before an evening of study, a busy household you're trying to study in the middle of, the blue sky of summer tempting you away from the computer screen. These things can pull focus and steer you off your learning journey. Then there's technical limitations. Sure, we all have to start somewhere, but learning new programs or how to use your computer for coding is daunting when starting out. It's important to recognize because it likely means you may move ahead slower than you'd like at times.

---

As anyone who has worked in a team will tell you, nothing is more challenging than struggling through a brainstorming session in silence. You're trying to be productive but no one wants to speak, contribute or even smile. I've overcome this scenario by splitting people off into pairs and having them write ideas down which I then read out once the whole group reassembled. I have seen people successfully add an element of fun into this 'boring' activity like adding a quiz at the top of the session, having music on for ten minutes in the background before starting. Working with others requires trust. These kinds of options are available when facing resistance working individually with someone - if possible, work for a period alone and then come together to discuss, don't jump into work straight away and chat about life for ten minutes. Anyway you can relax people's egos or remove the sense of judgement in the room, you should be open to trying.
