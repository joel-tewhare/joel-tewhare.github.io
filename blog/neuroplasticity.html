Better understanding our brain's power to learn and the 'Growth Mindset'

20 March 2025

Neuroplasticity. The word sounds as if it belongs in a Marvel movie, and why not? It's kind of fun to think of it like a real life super power, one we can wield in the face of life's challenges. Also, like any good blockbuster, there's a leading role - one you get to play while shaping your plastic, fantastic brain.

Neuroplasticity highlights our brain's ability to change our neural pathways, create new connections and organise our brains to better learn and behave differently. The research is a convincing case against the old understanding that our brain structure is more or less fixed from childhood.

The biggest benefits to people understanding this, in my opinion, are confidence and comfort. The first comes when we realise, while mastering a skill seems impossible when starting, the path to learning something deeply requires very achievable processes - repetition, reflection for example. The second is a more spiritual effect and the reason why research also shows that just explaining someone's brain to them in this way has an empowering effect, because they realise that intelligence is not just a predetermined trait.

Understanding neuroplasticity offers the chance to approach this course with three goals in mind:

  • Learning - improving my understanding with practice, review and putting information in a familiar context to create strong connections I can more easily relate to.
  • Personal growth - connecting what we're learning in this course with some other aspect of life that might be relevant or of benefit.
  • Building resilience - working towards 'practise making permanent' for as many skills as possible while understanding which exercises offer the most efficient way to achieve this.

If you find this topic interesting, check out: "Neuroplasticity: How the brain changes with learning" from the International Bureau of Education. Half way down, there's an outline of some study done on 'Neuroplasticity in Action' and a section on the story of Einstein's brain. Both are an easy and worthwhile read.


This scene from Bridesmaids is not the most academic of clips, but it's a funny segway into talking about the 'growth mindset', a concept based on what we've discussed so far - that our brain is capable of being shaped and changed over time.

Individuals who believe their talents can be developed through hard work, good strategies and input from others, have a growth mindset. I have heard this term thrown around before but after some further reading, my understanding changed slightly as to why it can be so effective. It's not that this mindset is made up of praise and reward for your effort alone. It's not that a growth mindset always equals good things happening. The value of this approach is how you experience the learning journey. Rather than a fixed mindset and viewing your skills as innate gifts, you will often achieve more with a growth mindset as you are less worried about looking smart and put more energy into learning. A comfortability for making mistakes or taking risks that might not work out - these are two important traits of a growth mindset I hope to have moving forward.

See Pete Bregman's write-up in the Harvard Business Review: "Why You Need to Fail". His writing style is very relatable and he shares good wit and wisdom while discussing this topic.

This all ties in nicely with my overall Learning Plan. Using methods that leverage our brain's neuroplasticity give me confidence in being able to level up my learning, particularly as we head towards Bootcamp. Demonstrating a growth mindset along this journey makes it easier to tackle those areas I wanted to work on and will help me keep a healthy attitude towards my progress overall.
